City History

Explore Sevilla's rich history, from ancient roots to modern allure,
revealing the city's enduring charm through the ages.

Resurging stronger than ever.

In 1728, the Royal Tobacco Factory was established in Seville, shaping the city's future. By the 20th century, it played a crucial role in national politics and hosted the 1929 Ibero-American Exposition. After the Spanish Civil War, Seville faced tragic events but emerged as the capital of Andalusia. The 1992 Universal Exposition further propelled the city's significance. Today, Seville is a key city in Spain.

Good times for Seville.

Seville, historically invaded by the Almoravids and later the Almohads, reached its zenith under Almohad rule, with notable constructions like the Torre del Oro and the Giralda. In 1248, it was conquered by Fernando III, making it a capital. In the 16th century, significant buildings like the Cathedral were constructed, but it faced economic competition. The plague of 1649 and the Counter-Reformation had a detrimental impact, halving the population. The origins of the city.

Seville's origins are shrouded in legends, with theories linking it to Tharsis and attributing its foundation to Hercules. The city, originally named "Spal" by the Turdetani tribe, evolved through Phoenician, Greek, and Carthaginian influences. Roman rule began in 205 B.C., transforming it into "Iulia Romuela." After a Visigothic period marked by internal strife, Seville became a cultural hub with Saints Leander and Isidore. In 712, it fell to the Muslims, adopting the name Isbiliyya. Following the Cordoban Caliphate's decline, Seville became a taifa kingdom under the Banu Abbad dynasty.
